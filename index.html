// how i build

Most automation fails quietly. Mine isn't allowed to.

[IDEMPOTENT]

Every retry is harmless. Deterministic IDs, upserts keyed on stable references — a double-submitted form or a network retry can never create a duplicate contact or inflate a revenue metric.

[BOUNDARY]

PII is hashed where it enters. Customer data is normalised and SHA-256 hashed in one audited place; downstream code, payloads, and logs never see plaintext. Data minimisation as architecture, not policy.

[FAIL-LOUD]

Failures page a human. Enrichment may fail quietly — a lead without enrichment beats a lost lead. The CRM write may not. Knowing which is which is most of the design work.

[ROI-FIRST]

I automate what makes money. Eight years close to revenue — business development, paid acquisition, lead ops — means I build the workflow that moves the P&L, not the one that demos well.

// about

Marketing taught me what to build. Security taught me how.

Most automation specialists learned the tools first and the business later. I did it the other way around — and it shows in everything I ship.

I spent three years in Dhaka closing ~£90,000 design contracts before I ever wrote a workflow, then moved to London and ran paid acquisition where every pound had to defend itself: ~3× blended ROAS, £700 cost per enrolment against £2,000 of value. So when iOS 14 quietly erased a fifth of our conversion data, I didn't raise a ticket and wait. I learned Meta's Conversions API, wrote the Python integration myself, and got our attribution back. That project changed my career — it's where "marketer who uses automation" became "engineer who understands marketing."

My MSc in Computer Network & System Security is the other half of the story. Automation touches the two things a business can least afford to get wrong — money and customer data — so I build GDPR compliance, secure key handling and auditability into the architecture, not the documentation. The proof is public: both repositories below are tested, documented around their design decisions, and built the way I'd build them for production. Because they were.

The part that doesn't show up in a repo: I've trained sceptical, non-technical teams to actually adopt this stuff. Automation that the team works around is just expensive shelf-ware — I build systems people trust, and I bring them along.
